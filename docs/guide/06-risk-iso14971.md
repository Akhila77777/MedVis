# Module 6: Risk Management (ISO 14971)

> **Status: ready.** This module builds a small **hazard analysis / risk file** for the
> needle-planning workflow, linking each risk back to the Module 5 requirements (and
> through them to the Capella functions and the code) that control it. It closes the loop:
> *what could go wrong* → *what the system does about it* → *proof it does it*.

> **Honesty note.** This is a **learning-scale** risk analysis, not a regulatory risk
> management file. There's no formal risk policy, no production data, no clinical input.
> It demonstrates that you can think in ISO 14971 terms — hazards, the risk chain, risk
> controls, residual risk — not that this device has been through risk management.

## What is this?

**ISO 14971** is *the* international standard for **risk management of medical devices**.
Its core idea: you cannot make a device with zero risk, so you must *systematically*
identify what could harm someone, estimate how bad and how likely, reduce it as far as
reasonably practicable, and then judge whether the remaining (residual) risk is acceptable
against the device's benefit.

The central artifact is a **hazard analysis table** — one row per way the device could
contribute to harm, each with its causes, severity, probability, and the controls that
reduce it.

## Regulatory context (where ISO 14971 sits)

- **EU MDR 2017/745** requires a documented **risk management system** across the device
  lifecycle (Annex I, GSPR §3) and that risks be reduced "as far as possible." ISO 14971 is
  the **harmonised standard** you use to show you've done that.
- **IEC 62304** (software lifecycle) hooks into it: software items that can contribute to a
  hazardous situation get a **software safety class**, and software risk control measures
  must be specified and verified (§7). The Class B/C decision in Module 5 *comes from* this
  risk thinking.
- **ISO 24971** is the official guidance on *how* to apply ISO 14971 (worked examples, scale
  definitions).

So the flow is: **ISO 14971 finds the hazards → risk controls become requirements (Module
5) → requirements are realized by functions (Module 4) and verified by tests/reviews.**

## The risk chain (the single most important mental model)

People conflate these three words; ISO 14971 keeps them strictly separate:

> **Hazard** → (sequence of events) → **Hazardous situation** → **Harm**

- **Hazard** — a *potential* source of harm. ("The planned needle path passes through a
  blood vessel.")
- **Hazardous situation** — the circumstance where a person is *exposed* to the hazard.
  ("The clinician advances the needle along that path believing it is clear.")
- **Harm** — the actual injury. ("Vessel laceration → internal haemorrhage.")
- **Sequence of events** — what has to happen to get from hazard to harm (e.g. *software
  tests the wrong segment* **and** *clinician doesn't catch it on imaging*).

Why it matters: **risk controls attack different links in this chain.** The intersection
test attacks the hazard; the clinician review attacks the sequence of events; the clear
red/green result attacks exposure in the hazardous situation.

## How software risk is special (a key honesty point)

ISO 14971 estimates risk as **Severity × Probability**, where probability is split into
**P1** (the hazardous situation occurs) × **P2** (it then leads to harm). But software faults
are **systematic, not random** — a bug either is or isn't there; it doesn't fail "1 in 10⁶
hours" like a resistor. So:

> For software you generally **cannot assign a meaningful failure probability.** ISO 14971 /
> IEC 62304 handle this by assuming the software failure *can occur* (worst-case
> probability) and driving safety from **severity + risk controls** instead. This is exactly
> why IEC 62304's software safety class is essentially severity-based.

Being able to say *that* in an interview ("you don't probability-rate a bug; you assume it
can happen and control by severity") signals real understanding.

## Risk control hierarchy (ISO 14971 §7, in priority order)

You must apply controls in this order, not just pick the easy one:

1. **Inherently safe design** — design out the hazard. *(e.g. REQ-006: the software computes
   the intersection automatically rather than relying on the eye.)*
2. **Protective measures** — guards/alarms in the device or process. *(e.g. REQ-009: never
   show "Clear" unless a check actually ran; REQ-007: force a result before proceeding.)*
3. **Information for safety** — labelling, warnings, training. *(e.g. REQ-008 colour-coding,
   REQ-010 naming the tested segment; the intended-purpose statement "planning aid only".)*

"Information for safety" is the **weakest** control — a warning a tired clinician can ignore.
Strong risk files lean on 1 and 2 and use 3 only to top up.

## Key terms

- **Hazard / hazardous situation / harm** — the chain above.
- **Severity (S)** — how bad the harm is.
- **Probability of occurrence of harm (P)** — often P1 × P2.
- **Risk** — combination of severity and probability (here S × P, or a risk matrix).
- **Risk control measure** — what reduces a risk (mapped to a requirement).
- **Residual risk** — risk remaining *after* controls.
- **Benefit-risk** — judgement that the residual risk is acceptable given the clinical
  benefit.
- **Risk management file** — the collected record (this is what we're emulating).

## Step-by-step

Output: a hazard analysis under `risk-analysis/`.

1. **Define scales.** A simple Severity scale and Probability scale, plus an acceptability
   rule (see below).
2. **Identify hazards** specific to *this* workflow (vessel puncture, stale/misleading
   result, missed target, no-result-on-bad-input).
3. **For each, write the chain:** hazard → cause/sequence → hazardous situation → harm.
4. **Estimate initial risk** (S, P → risk level) *before* controls.
5. **List risk controls**, each tagged with its hierarchy level and **linked to a Module 5
   requirement ID** (this is the traceability payoff).
6. **Estimate residual risk** after controls, and state acceptability.
7. **Assemble the table** and save under `risk-analysis/`.

### Example scales

**Severity (S):** 1 Negligible · 2 Minor · 3 Serious · 4 Critical · 5 Catastrophic
**Probability (P):** 1 Improbable · 2 Remote · 3 Occasional · 4 Probable · 5 Frequent
**Acceptability:** Risk = S × P → 1–4 Low (accept) · 5–9 Medium (reduce if practicable) ·
10–25 High (must reduce).

## Closing the loop

This is the moment the whole project becomes one connected argument:

> **ISO 14971 hazard** (vessel puncture) → **risk control** → **REQ-006 "determine
> intersection"** (Module 5) → **"Verify clearance" function** (Module 4) →
> **`checkClearance()`** with its unit tests (Module 3). Every link is a real artifact.

That end-to-end traceability — hazard to line of code, and back — is the single strongest
thing in this portfolio for a German medtech role.

## Checkpoint

You're done with Module 6 when `risk-analysis/` contains:

- Defined **severity / probability scales** and an acceptability rule.
- A **hazard table** with 3–4 hazards, each showing the full chain, initial risk, risk
  controls (tagged by hierarchy and **linked to REQ IDs**), and residual risk.
- At least the **vessel-puncture** hazard fully worked, tracing to REQ-006 and friends.
